To transmit electrical power over long distances, we do the following.

- First, at the power plant, transformers step up the voltage from the generator (for example, 11 kV) to very high voltages (for example, 400 kV).
	- Higher voltage means lower current for the same power, which reduces energy lost as heat in transmission lines.
	- This is because $P_{loss} = I^2V$, power loss is proportional to square of current. We raise voltage to lower current to reduce loss. Very high voltages can jump across air gaps or through insulation → sparks, fires, equipment failure. So 400kV is a good middle ground.

- Next, the high-voltage electricity travels through transmission lines, often supported by tall towers. Near towns or cities, step-down transformers reduce the voltage in stages, for example from 400 kV to 33 kV, then to 11 kV, and finally to a safe 230 V or 120 V for homes.
- Local distribution lines then carry the electricity to homes and businesses, with circuit breakers, fuses, and meters ensuring safety and monitoring.


- AC is used because it is easy to transform between high and low voltages using transformers. DC can be used, but as it is complex this is uncommon.
