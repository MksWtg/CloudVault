Prerequisites: [[AC Synchronous Motor]], [[Excitation]]

Also known as electrically excited synchronous motor.

A wound field synchronous motor has two main parts: the stator and the rotor.

The rotor contains field windings (coils of wire) instead of permanent magnets. These windings are supplied with DC current. The DC current produces a steady magnetic field in the rotor, creating north and south magnetic poles. The DC usually reaches the rotor either through slip rings and brushes or through a brushless exciter system.

One important detail is that a wound field synchronous motor normally cannot start by itself when connected directly to AC power. At standstill, the rotating stator field moves too quickly relative to the rotor, so the magnetic forces average out and produce little starting torque. Because of this, the motor usually needs some way to get close to synchronous speed first. This can be done with damper windings that let it start like an induction motor, with a separate starting motor, or by using a variable frequency drive that slowly ramps the frequency up.

Once the rotor reaches near synchronous speed, the rotor magnetic field locks to the stator rotating field and the motor runs smoothly at constant speed with essentially zero slip. The strength of the rotor field can be adjusted by changing the DC excitation current, which allows control of power factor and reactive power in large industrial systems.
