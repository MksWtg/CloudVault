
Identity politics is a way of thinking about politics where people organize around shared aspects of their identity such as race, gender, religion, sexuality, or ethnicity to advocate for their interests, rights, and recognition.

The idea is that people who share certain experiences (for example, discrimination or marginalization) often have common political concerns. So instead of focusing only on broad categories like class or national interest, identity politics highlights how different groups may be affected differently by laws, policies, or social norms.

The term became widely used in the late 20th century, especially in movements like:
- Civil rights movements (focused on race)
- Feminist movements (focused on gender)
- LGBTQ+ rights movements (focused on sexual orientation and gender identity)

Supporters argue that identity politics is important because:
- It gives a voice to groups that have historically been overlooked or excluded
- It helps address specific injustices that affect particular communities
- It can build solidarity among people with shared experiences

Critics, on the other hand, argue that it can:
- Divide society into smaller groups rather than focusing on common ground
- Encourage people to see themselves primarily through a single identity
- Be used in ways that oversimplify complex issues
    
In practice, most modern political systems involve some mix of identity-based concerns and broader policy debates (like economics, healthcare, or education).
