Albert Szent-Györgyi discovered vitamin C and key aspects of cellular respiration, earning a Nobel Prize and transforming modern biochemistry.  
John von Neumann made foundational contributions to computer science, game theory, and nuclear strategy that shaped the modern digital and geopolitical world.  
Leó Szilárd conceived the nuclear chain reaction and helped initiate the Manhattan Project while later advocating for nuclear arms control.  
Edward Teller played a central role in developing the hydrogen bomb and influencing Cold War nuclear policy.  
Eugene Wigner advanced the theory of nuclear reactions and the role of symmetry in physics, earning a Nobel Prize.  
Imre Nagy became a symbol of resistance by leading Hungary during the 1956 uprising against Soviet control and was executed for it.  
Béla Bartók revolutionized classical music by integrating folk traditions into modern composition and advancing ethnomusicology.  
Zoltán Kodály transformed music education worldwide through the Kodály Method and preserved Hungarian folk music heritage.  
János Kádár ruled Hungary for decades after 1956, shaping a relatively liberal form of communism known as “Goulash Communism.”  
Dennis Gabor invented holography, a breakthrough in imaging science that earned him a Nobel Prize.
