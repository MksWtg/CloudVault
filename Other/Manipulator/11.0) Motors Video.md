With brushed commutation for DC motors, we have one DC steady state signal and we want to pass it through the three windings. We need to time this- each winding is offset by 120 degrees, so if we were to energize them all simultaneously, depending on the orientation of the windings some would generate a field in one direction with a certain torque, others would generate a different field with different torque and the symmetry would cancel out.

Instead we wire the windings in a "wye" configuration- that way the winding that is parallel with the stator field that will generate maximum torque gets the positive voltage, and the winding that is within 60 degrees of parallel the other way gets negative voltage. The connections are achieved via brushes. The process to energize the right winding as the rotor spins is known as commutation.

## Flywheel Diodes

The diodes in parallel with H-bridge MOSFETs are called flyback or freewheeling diodes, and they exist because of inductive loads, like motors.



Inductors resist sudden current changes A motor winding is basically an inductor.

When current through an inductor is suddenly interrupted (e.g., a MOSFET switches off), the inductor generates a voltage spike in the opposite direction to try to keep the current flowing (Lenz’s law).

Diodes provide a safe path for this current Without the diode, the voltage spike could damage the MOSFET.

The diode allows the inductor current to “freewheel” through the MOSFET leg safely until it naturally decays.

How it works in an H-bridge:

- Each MOSFET has a diode in parallel (usually built-in in modern MOSFETs).
- When you switch off a MOSFET, current from the motor can flow through the diode to the other MOSFET in the bridge, preventing voltage spikes and enabling regenerative paths.

- command: torque

- command: voltage
